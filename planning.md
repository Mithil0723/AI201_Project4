# Provenance Guard — Planning

This is the spec I wrote before building. It's the document I fed (section by
section) to an AI tool while generating the implementation, so it has to be
concrete: vague answers here would have produced vague code.

---

## The problem in one paragraph

Creative platforms can't easily tell whether a poem or blog post was written by
the person who posted it or generated by AI and passed off as their own. The
point isn't to police creativity — it's attribution and trust. Provenance Guard
is a backend any such platform can call: it takes a piece of text, runs it
through several independent detectors, combines them into a single confidence,
shows the reader an honest label, and gives the creator a way to push back if
the call was wrong.

---

## Architecture

### Submission flow

```
                 POST /submit  { text, creator_id }
                         |
                         v
            +---------------------------+
            |   run_ensemble(text)      |
            |                           |
   text --> |  signal 1: LLM (Groq)     | --score(0-1)--+
   text --> |  signal 2: stylometry     | --score(0-1)--+--> weighted
   text --> |  signal 3: lexical markers| --score(0-1)--+    combine
            +---------------------------+                     |
                                                              v
                                                   confidence (0-1)
                                                              |
                                                              v
                                              make_label(confidence)
                                              -> attribution + label text
                                                              |
                          +-----------------------------------+
                          v                                   v
                 write to submissions               write to audit_log
                 (status="classified")              (event="classified")
                          |                                   |
                          +-----------------+-----------------+
                                            v
                 JSON response: content_id, attribution, confidence,
                                label, per-signal scores
```

### Appeal flow

```
        POST /appeal  { content_id, creator_reasoning }
                         |
                         v
          look up submission by content_id  --(not found)--> 404
                         |
                         v
          UPDATE submissions SET status = "under_review"
                         |
                         v
          write audit_log row (event="appeal", status="under_review",
                               appeal_reasoning=..., original scores copied)
                         |
                         v
          JSON response: confirmation + original decision shown back
```

**Narrative.** On submit, the raw text fans out to three independent signals;
their scores are combined into one confidence, which decides the label, and both
the decision and a full audit row are written before the response goes back. On
appeal, the creator's `content_id` and reasoning come in, the submission's status
flips to `under_review`, and the appeal is logged right next to the original
decision so a human reviewer sees both in one place.

## Question 1 — Detection signals

Three signals (the third is the ensemble stretch feature). Each looks at a
genuinely different property of the text.

**Signal 1 — LLM semantic judgement (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`).**
What it measures: whether the text *reads* as human or machine — idea
development, voice, the presence of specific lived detail vs. evenly-hedged
generic prose. Output: a float `ai_likelihood` in 0–1 (asked for as JSON,
temperature 0 for stability). Blind spot: confident, fluent AI text and lightly
human-edited AI can fool it; it can also be over-eager to call polished human
writing "AI."

**Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python).**
What it measures: how *uniform* the writing is, via three sub-metrics —
sentence-length burstiness (coefficient of variation), type-token ratio
(vocabulary diversity, length-corrected), and punctuation variety. AI prose is
smooth and even; human writing is burstier and reaches for dashes, ellipses,
parentheses. Output: a float 0–1. Blind spot: short texts (too few sentences to
measure variance), and formal humans who naturally write in even, tidy sentences.

**Signal 3 — Lexical AI-marker density (pure Python).**
What it measures: how often the text uses words and transition phrases that LLMs
overuse ("furthermore," "moreover," "it is important to note," "delve,"
"tapestry," "leverage," "robust," "paradigm"…), scaled per 100 words so a long
human essay isn't punished for one "however." Output: a float 0–1. Blind spot: a
human who genuinely writes formal academic prose will trip it; an AI told to
"write casually" will dodge it.

**Why these three are distinct:** signal 1 is *semantic* (meaning), signal 2 is
*structural* (statistics of the prose), signal 3 is *lexical* (word choice). They
fail in different situations, so combining them is more informative than any one
alone — and where they disagree, the combined score sits near 0.5, which is the
honest answer.

**Combining them.** Weighted average. Base weights: LLM 0.50, stylometry 0.30,
lexical 0.20 — the LLM carries the most weight because semantic judgement is the
strongest single signal, while the two cheap structural signals act as an
independent cross-check that can pull a confident LLM call back toward
"uncertain." If the LLM is unavailable (no key / no network), its weight is
redistributed: stylometry 0.60, lexical 0.40, re-normalised so a missing signal
never silently drags confidence to zero. A transparency-only majority "vote"
(how many of the three individually exceed 0.5) is also reported, but it does
not drive the decision — the weighted score does.

## Question 2 — Uncertainty representation

**What `confidence` means.** It is the system's estimated probability that the
text is **AI-generated**:

- `0.0` → we're confident a human wrote it
- `0.5` → genuinely undecided
- `1.0` → we're confident it's AI

I decided what 0.5 should mean to a user *first* (maximal honesty: "we don't
know") and built the scoring to land there when the signals disagree, rather
than forcing a binary flip.

**Mapping raw signals → calibrated score.** Each signal already returns 0–1 on
the same "ai-likelihood" axis, so the weighted average is itself the calibrated
confidence. The sub-metrics inside stylometry are individually clamped to 0–1
and mixed (burstiness 0.55, ttr 0.20, punctuation 0.25) before they leave the
signal, so no single quirky metric can dominate.

**Thresholds (deliberately asymmetric):**

| confidence (AI-likelihood) | attribution    |
|----------------------------|----------------|
| `< 0.40`                   | `likely_human` |
| `0.40 – 0.70`              | `uncertain`    |
| `> 0.70`                   | `likely_ai`    |

The "likely AI" bar is high (0.70) and the uncertain band is wide on purpose.
On a writing platform, **falsely flagging a real person's work as AI is the worse
error**, so the system would rather say "uncertain" than accuse. A 0.51 lands in
"uncertain" with a hedged label; a 0.95 lands in "likely AI" with a direct one —
meaningfully different outcomes, which is the whole point.

---

## Question 3 — Transparency label design

Three variants, keyed off the thresholds above. Exact text the reader sees
(the `{pct}` values are filled from the confidence at runtime):

**High-confidence AI** (`confidence > 0.70`):
> 🤖 Likely AI-generated. Our automated analysis estimates a {ai_pct}% chance
> this text was produced with generative AI. This is an estimate, not a verdict —
> the creator can appeal if they believe it's wrong.

**High-confidence human** (`confidence < 0.40`):
> ✍️ Likely human-written. Our automated analysis found no strong signs of AI
> generation (estimated {human_pct}% human-written). This is an estimate, not a
> guarantee.

**Uncertain** (`0.40 ≤ confidence ≤ 0.70`):
> ❓ Uncertain origin. Our analysis couldn't confidently tell whether a human or
> AI wrote this (roughly {ai_pct}% toward AI, {human_pct}% toward human). We're
> flagging it as inconclusive rather than guessing.

Every variant says it's an *estimate*, never a certainty, and the AI/uncertain
variants point to the appeal path.

## Question 4 — Appeals workflow

**Who can appeal:** the creator of a classified submission (in this prototype,
anyone holding the `content_id`; a production system would tie this to the
authenticated creator account).

**What they provide:** the `content_id` and free-text `creator_reasoning`.

**What the system does on receipt:**
1. Looks up the submission; returns `404` if the `content_id` is unknown.
2. Updates its status from `classified` to `under_review`.
3. Writes an `audit_log` row with `event="appeal"`, the creator's reasoning, and
   a copy of the original scores/attribution so the decision and the challenge
   sit together.
4. Returns a confirmation that echoes the original decision back to the creator.

Automated re-classification is intentionally **not** done — an appeal is a
request for *human* review.

**What a reviewer sees in the queue:** every `audit_log` row where
`status="under_review"`, showing the original attribution, confidence, all three
signal scores, and the creator's stated reasoning — enough to make a call without
re-running anything.

## Question 5 — Anticipated edge cases (where it does poorly)

1. **Formal, tidy human writing (e.g. an academic abstract).** Even sentence
   lengths trip the stylometry burstiness metric and formal vocabulary trips the
   lexical signal, so two of three signals push toward AI. This is exactly why
   the LLM weight is highest and the "likely AI" bar is at 0.70 — in testing, the
   monetary-policy sample lands at ~0.38 (`likely_human`) instead of being
   wrongly flagged. Still a real weakness: a denser formal passage could cross
   into "uncertain."

2. **Short text (a haiku, a one-line caption).** With one or two sentences there
   isn't enough material to measure sentence-length variance or vocabulary
   diversity, so stylometry is near-blind. The code damps the stylometry and
   lexical influence for texts under ~30–40 words, but a short, polished AI
   couplet and a short human one can be genuinely indistinguishable — the right
   answer there is "uncertain," and that's what the wide middle band produces.

3. **Lightly human-edited AI.** A person who runs AI text through a quick rewrite
   removes the obvious lexical markers and adds a little burstiness, so all three
   signals soften — it should, and usually does, land mid-range "uncertain"
   rather than a confident call.

## AI Tool Plan

How I used an AI tool across the three implementation milestones.

**M3 — submission endpoint + first signal.**
Provide: the *Detection signals* section (signal 1) + the architecture diagram.
Ask for: a Flask skeleton with a `POST /submit` stub and the `signal_llm(text)`
function returning a 0–1 score as JSON. Verify: call `signal_llm` directly on a
few inputs and confirm the return shape matches the spec (a float, plus an
`available` flag) before wiring it into the route.

**M4 — second signal + confidence scoring.**
Provide: *Detection signals* (signals 2–3) + *Uncertainty representation* + the
diagram. Ask for: `signal_stylometry` and `signal_lexical` plus `run_ensemble`
that combines them with the documented weights and thresholds. Verify: run all
four calibration inputs and confirm clearly-AI vs clearly-human produce clearly
different scores, and that the implemented thresholds match the table above (AI
tools sometimes drift from the specified ranges — check, don't assume).

**M5 — production layer.**
Provide: *Transparency label design* + *Appeals workflow* + the diagram. Ask for:
`make_label(confidence)` mapping scores to the three exact variants, and the
`POST /appeal` endpoint. Verify: submit inputs that hit each band and confirm all
three labels are reachable; file an appeal and confirm status flips to
`under_review` and the audit log captures it.

## Stretch feature — Ensemble detection (3+ signals)

Done as part of the core build rather than bolted on: the pipeline runs **three**
signals (LLM, stylometry, lexical) and combines them with the documented weighted
scheme (0.50 / 0.30 / 0.20, re-normalised if the LLM is unavailable), plus a
reported majority vote for transparency. See the *Detection signals* section for
the weighting rationale and the README for the implementation write-up.
